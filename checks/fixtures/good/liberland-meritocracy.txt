In this episode you will learn two things: how Liberland's direct meritocracy
actually works, and why its founder thinks voting is the wrong tool for
governing a nation.

Vit Jedlicka proclaimed Liberland on the western bank of the Danube on the 13th
of April 2015. The seven square kilometres between Croatia and Serbia were
unclaimed — a terra nullius left over from a border dispute that neither country
wanted to own. Within a year, half a million people had applied for citizenship.

The system Jedlicka designed is not a democracy. It is a direct meritocracy.
Citizens earn voting power by contributing to the community — building
infrastructure, providing services, or creating economic value. One
contribution, one vote weight. No hereditary privilege. No party machines.

The blockchain handles the accounting. Every contribution is recorded
on-chain, and the merit score determines your weight in referendums. The
counter-argument is obvious: who decides what counts as a contribution? The
answer, for now, is Liberland's council — a small group Jedlicka appointed
directly. That centralisation is the weak spot, and Jedlicka knows it.

Liberland has no income tax. Its revenue model relies on transaction fees,
donations, and the appreciation of its land. Whether that scales past a few
hundred residents is an open question.

What Liberland proves is that the mechanics of governance are up for grabs.
The nation-state model has been frozen since Westphalia in 1648. Jedlicka is
betting that technology — blockchain, smart contracts, merit-based voting —
can unfreeze it. The experiment is barely a decade old. The results are not
yet in, but the question Liberland asks is one that every taxpayer should be
asking: who consented to the system you live under, and what would you change
if you could start from scratch?
